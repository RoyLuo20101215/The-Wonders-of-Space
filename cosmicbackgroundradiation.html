Cosmic Background Radiation

Overview

The Cosmic Microwave Background (CMB) radiation is a faint glow permeating the universe, representing the afterglow of the Big Bang that occurred approximately 13.8 billion years ago. It is the oldest light in the universe, providing a snapshot of the infant cosmos when it was just about 380,000 years old. The CMB is a critical piece of evidence supporting the Big Bang theory, as it reveals information about the universe's early conditions, structure, and evolution. Today, it is studied extensively to gain insights into the universe's expansion, composition, and the formation of large-scale structures.

History

The history of CMB radiation began with theoretical predictions in the 1940s, particularly by George Gamow and his colleagues, who explored the implications of the Big Bang model. The radiation was serendipitously discovered in 1965 by Arno Penzias and Robert Wilson while they were conducting radio astronomy experiments. Their detection of a uniform background noise led to the realization that it was the long-sought after remnant radiation from the Big Bang. This discovery earned them the Nobel Prize in Physics in 1978 and provided substantial evidence for the Big Bang theory, transforming our understanding of the universe's origins.

Origin

The origin of the CMB can be traced back to the early universe, when it was a hot, dense plasma filled with particles, including protons, electrons, and photons. As the universe expanded and cooled, these particles began to combine to form hydrogen atoms during a period known as recombination, approximately 380,000 years after the Big Bang. At this point, photons could travel freely without being scattered by charged particles, resulting in the decoupling of matter and radiation. The CMB is thus a relic of this time, redshifted to microwave wavelengths due to the continued expansion of the universe. Today, it is measured in various wavelengths, with its detailed fluctuations providing valuable information about the universe's composition, structure, and fate.
