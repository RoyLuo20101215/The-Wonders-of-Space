The Big Bang

Overview

The theory of how the universe expanded upon the intital state. First proposed in 1931, the Big Bang Theory explains the evolution of the universe. The model offers a comprehensive explanation for a broad range of pheonomena's in our universe such as the abundence of chemical elements, CMB and hubbles law. It still remains as one of the most known and accurate theories of the beggining of this universe.

Expansion of the Universe

One of the most prominent theories of the universe is the cosmic expansion. The expansion of the universe is the increase in distance between gravitationally unbound parts of the observable universe with time. It is an intrinsic expansion, so it does not mean that the universe expands "into" anything or that space exists "outside" it.

Timeline of The Big Bang

Singularity

The Big Bang theory starts with a state of singularity, a point of infinite density and temperature, where all matter and energy were compressed into an incredibly small space. At this moment, the laws of physics as we understand them break down. Time and space themselves did not exist before this singularity. As the universe began to expand, it moved away from this infinitely dense point, marking the beginning of both time and the universe. This concept is rooted in general relativity, but scientists are still investigating what could have caused the singularity.

Cosmic Inflation

Following the singularity, the universe experienced a brief but dramatic period of inflation. This phase, occurring fractions of a second after the Big Bang, saw the universe expand exponentially, growing faster than the speed of light. During inflation, the universe expanded by a factor of at least 10^26, smoothing out any irregularities and laying the foundation for the large-scale structure we observe today. This rapid expansion also helped to explain why the universe is relatively uniform in all directions, a problem known as the horizon problem.

Cooling

As the universe expanded, it began to cool. Initially, temperatures were so high that matter existed only in the form of elementary particles, but as it cooled, these particles began to combine, forming protons, neutrons, and electrons. Around 380,000 years after the Big Bang, the temperature dropped enough for protons and electrons to combine and form neutral hydrogen atoms. This event, known as recombination, allowed photons to travel freely through space, leading to the formation of the Cosmic Microwave Background Radiation (CMB)—the oldest light we can observe in the universe.

Structure Formation

After the cooling phase, slight density fluctuations that occurred during inflation became seeds for the formation of galaxies, stars, and other cosmic structures. Over time, gravity caused these denser regions to collapse, forming the first galaxies and clusters. This process of structure formation was gradual, with galaxies slowly forming and merging into larger systems. The distribution of these structures traces the "cosmic web" of dark matter that underlies the visible universe.

Cosmic Expansion

Cosmic expansion continues today, as galaxies move away from one another, a process that began after inflation. Observations show that the universe's expansion is accelerating due to a mysterious force known as dark energy. This discovery, made in the late 1990s, changed our understanding of the universe's fate. While the exact nature of dark energy remains unknown, it plays a crucial role in driving the universe's ongoing expansion, which is expected to continue indefinitely.
